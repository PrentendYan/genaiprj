Can LLMs Audit
Quant Research?

An adversarial AI reviewer reads a backtest the way a careful quant would, and looks for the one line that turns a losing strategy into a winning chart.

45 labeled cases 5 reviewer adapters 90 live model calls, 0 failures

The Question

AI tools can write a backtest in minutes, and that speed is exactly what makes research mistakes easier to miss. A shift in the wrong direction, a normalization step before the split, a random shuffle of a time series, a Sharpe ratio computed gross of costs: none of these errors announces itself, and each produces a backtest that looks profitable.

This project asks whether an adversarial AI reviewer, one model checking another model's work, can catch those mistakes reliably enough to be worth putting into a research workflow. It builds two designs, DARF and CORAX, into a reproducible benchmark and measures them against a naive rule baseline.

The Benchmark

Forty-five labeled audit cases, each a small piece of real research material, drawn from BTC data, multi-stock equity data, and real tutorial notebooks. Five failure modes are covered, alongside eleven clean controls written as deliberate near-misses, so an over-eager reviewer is exposed as precisely as a careless one.

Results

Five adapters, full 45-case benchmark. Two run offline as deterministic scans; two run live, spawning a real model per case.

The baseline fails systematically: with no rule for normalization or unsupported claims, it misses those categories entirely. AI review closes the gap, lifting recall from 0.56 to as high as 1.00. But the two strongest adapters sit at opposite ends of a precision–recall tradeoff. There is no single best reviewer; the right one depends on whether a setting can better tolerate a missed problem or a false alarm.

Three Findings

01

A live model caught what no scanner could

One backtest declares a transaction-cost variable, then never uses it. Four of five adapters miss this dead store. Only darf-live reads the data flow and catches it, the clearest evidence that semantic review recovers errors rule-based scanning structurally cannot.

02

Offline DARF and CORAX are indistinguishable

On this benchmark the two offline adapters share a scanner and produce identical results. The mechanism difference between the designs only becomes visible once a real model is in the loop.

03

When four reviewers outvote the label

One case annotated clean is flagged by all four non-baseline adapters. When independent reviewers, across two model families, unanimously contradict a label, the benchmark is using their agreement to surface an ambiguity in its own annotations.

Where AI Stands

Where it helps

A large, real gain over naive rule checking, concentrated exactly where rules are weakest: recall climbs from 0.56 to 1.00, and a live model catches semantic errors no deterministic scan detects.

Where it does not

It does not settle the DARF-versus-CORAX design question. Offline, the adversarial machinery meant to differentiate them is never exercised, so the benchmark compares coverage, not design.

Where a human is needed

Taxonomy. Whether random-split and lookahead are one issue or two, and how to rule on an ambiguous case, are annotation choices. AI can expose the boundary; it cannot define it.
